    PETE BELASCO Get It Together (Universal)
    Very rarely will you find an album this hot that combines soul & jazz; Pete Belasco has done just that. "Get It Together" is faintly reminiscent of George Michael's & Michael Frank's dreamy, soulful vocals with a dash of Georgie Fame's famous organ style thrown richin. Sade lovers' will enjoy the silky smoothness of "Love Train". Th blends of saxophones, organ & soft vocals create an ambience that is almost unique to Belasco. Sit back with your coffee and let this sexy album wash over you. Gazz

    RENEE GEYER Sweet Life (BMG)
    If in-case I had forgotten how strong and mesmerising the smouldering emotion of Renee Geyer's husky voice is. The album 'Sweet Life', not only reminded me once more that Geyer who was hailed in the 70's as Australia's First Lady of Soul/Funk was and still is one of the finest voices that will ever grace my album collection. From the first track Geyer's exceptional voice will draw you in, I turned up the volume, poured a good $10.00 Red and joined in all the choruses. There are 10 original tracks and a good cover of, 'I'm Gonna Make You Love Me'. The most impressive aspect of this album is that four of the most powerful songs on the album Geyer wrote herself, illustrating an emerging talent that possibly derives inspiration from her songwriter friend Paul Kelly, who co-produced this album with Joe Camilleri and has written and produced some great numbers for Renee Geyer on this album and previously on the CD, 'Difficult Woman', which he also produced.     Tori Amos From The Choirgirl Hotel (Warner)
    I've had a soft spot for the quirky red headed piano player- singer/songwriter ever since hearing the single "Silent All These Years" from her Little Earthquakes album. Now Tori Amos has made a very classy, more contemporary record than any of her previous efforts to date. One can never be sure whether she's playful girl next door or avenging angel! What ever it is, she's passionate and animated in her devotion to her subject. From the prickly self doubt of "Spark" to affirmations of strength to the suffering in "Black Dove(January)",Tori wends her way through a gambit of emotions. "Black Dove (January)" and another song called "Playboy Mommy" seem to make allusions to the loss of a child. There are the usual references to the illusions of love and happiness in songs like "Jackie's Strength", "Hotel" and "Northern Lad". Tori abandons the minimalist approach of 1996s Boys For Pele for a more industrial sound on From The Choirgirl Hotel. With a lacing of ballads like the beautifully orchestrated "Black Dove (January)". From The Choirgirl Hotel is produced by Tori and she provides some great piano work here in. It's difficult to pick favourite tracks as each one has something to offer. Kate Bush still seems to have an influence in Tori's vocal style, especially on the driving "She's Your Cocaine". There's no doubt that Tori Amos is still able to create inventive and thought provoking music of a standard consistent with any of her past work. Micheil Reid

    Jazzman Garrett scores at Catalina
    Kenny Garrett (Jazz; Catalina Bar & Grill, Hollywood; 120 seats)
    By Phil Gallo
    HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - The approach is not unlike that of a major league hitter who likes fastballs on the first pitch: the action is immediate and the results often scintillating, the rare breathtaking moment in a baseball game. The alto saxophonist Kenny Garrett stood sideways to audience, whispered a count and bam! Like hitter smacking the ball toward the bleachers, he and the band were off and running with abandon for 15 minutes on Herbie Hancock's ``One Finger Snap.'' Having come to prominence in one of Miles Davis' later bands, Garrett, a consistent poll winner for his alto saxophone playing, is a fireball in a live setting. On his recent Warner Bros. outing, ``Simply Said,'' and the handful that preceded it, by contrast, he stresses melody and coherence, venturing into the light side of rap, funk and the music of John Coltrane. On this, the second set of the second night, Garrett displayed a wondrous sense of invention as he tackled Coltrane's ``Giant Steps'' with far more intensity than he did on record, venturing unaccompanied with focus intact and showcasing his band, particularly the light touch of pianist Nick Smith. Venturing into his own compositions, Garrett began to simmer on numbers such as ``Charlie Brown Goes to South Africa,'' which is exactly that -- Vince Guaraldi with funky poly-rhythms -- and his spin on beat poetry, ``Back Where You Started.'' Garrett's key foil is drummer Marcus Baylor, who kept time within a flurry of patterns that were sometimes too sprawling for the music while his solos were welcome flights of hard-nosed drumming. But the evening got an added spark when ace trumpeter Roy Hargrove approached the stage from the side and began a duel with Garrett that continued through the final three numbers. Hargrove clearly pushed Garrett without upstaging him, a demonstration of the rewards only jazz can deliver when the musical environment is perfect. Presented inhouse. Band: Kenny Garrett, Nick Smith, Nat Reeves, Marcus Baylor. Guest: Roy Hargrove. Opened July 27, 1999, reviewed July 28, closes Aug. 1. Reuters/Variety (Thank you Peter Andrews @ Warner Brothers for this news article)

    ROLF JARDEMARK - Biography
    "Jardemark is unable to resist his need to play jazz" - writes a critic from the British Jazz Journal: After listening to the CD Guitarland most everyone is inclined to agree. In the Danish 'Special Jazz' you find: "Jardemark plays 'real' jazz guitar and has a delightful and warm sound". Rolf Jardemark, born 1960 in Gothenburg, Sweden. Picked up the guitar around the age of twelve. After a few years of self studies he enrolled at music high school. The lively jazz scene of Gothenburg, with musicians as e.g. guitarist Ulf Wakenius and the jazz club "Artdur" (now renamed "Nefertiti"), made a strong impression. In 1977 Jardemark and fellow guitarist Per Hovensjö founded a duo, still active today. After graduating from Gothenburg College of Music in 1985, Jardemark has been seen in many different musical constellations; such as jazz-rock groups, big bands, and "acid jazz." Rolf Jardemark has recorded and performed together with Lars Danielsson, Erik Norström, Michael Ruff, Bohuslän Big Band, Nils Landgren, David Wilczewski, Al Harewood, Nils-Bertil Dahlander (Bert Dale), to name a few. Jardemark has played most of the jazz clubs in Sweden, both with his own group and as a side man. He appeared with Rune Gustafsson on the nationally televised show "Jazzduellen" (The Jazz Duel) in 1994. The collaboration with Gustafsson has continued since, both with a quartet and with Bohuslän Big Band. Rolf Jardemark has released the following solo albums: Soft Landing (Liphone LICD 3095), Jungle Crunch (Imogena CD 027), and Guitarland (IGCD 044). The latter with Lars Danielsson on bass, and Anders Kjellberg on drums. "Guitarland" received great reviews both in Sweden and abroad. Jardemark´s new CD "Further Adventures in Guitarland" (Imogena CD 067) was released in -97 with Yasuhito Mori on bass, and Terje Sundby on drums. Guest appearances on some cuts by Thomas Gustafsson on saxophone, and Erik Gullbransson vocals. In addition to his own group, Jardemark is also a member of Jan Zirk Quartet. String Jazz, Nov 1996 - Five Stars. Considering the size of the country (or maybe the size of population), Sweden keeps on producing some fine jazz guitarists. From the superb Rune Gustafsson through to Ulf Wakenius and Peter Almqvist and now a new name - that of Rolf Jardemark. Jardemark comes from the school of Wes Montgomery, Kenny Burrell and Pat Martino and you can heartraces of of each of these fine players on this CD (Burrell on Linneus, Wes of course on Four on Six and Martino particularly on There´s no greater love). But Jardemark is his own man (you might expect this with the above mentioned as his spiritual mentors) - tasteful, melodic, always probing. From the hard edged post bop of No Time to Waste, he sinks into a fine finger-style rendition of the lovely In a sentimental mood. With the rock solid and swinging supprt of the superb bassist Lars Danielsson and drummer Anders Kjellberg, Jardemark shows on this CD that Europe has produced yet another fine jazz guitarist in the mainstream tradition. A very fine CD indeed and a must for anyone in this style.*****

     PLAYING BY HEART/original soundtrack album (Universal)
    "No matter how many times one has done it--and I've done over 80 pictures now--it doesn't make any difference: the most favourite part is when you've finished writing in detail for several weeks and you have maybe an hour's music that nobody's ever really heard before--except for bits and pieces--and then you go into the studio at 10 o'clock on Monday morning or whatever it is. There are 70 or 80 of the finest musicians. You bring the baton down and this thing starts to come to life. It's the best feeling. That and sex, and I don't know which comes first." So said British film composer John Barry--born in York but long a resident of New York--a couple of years back. I'd bet that the position hasn't changed. Famous for his lush, sweeping (and Oscar-winning) scores for films such as "Out of Africa" and "Dances With Wolves," Barry's original fondness for jazz has deeply influenced efforts like "The Cotton Club," "Body Heat," and more lately Barry's soundtrack for the IMAX film "Across the Sea of Time." Latest out is another movie track, that for "Playing By Heart," an interesting-sounding 1990s relationships piece with a definitely interesting cast. Included are Sean Connery, Gena Rowlands, Gillian Anderson, Dennis Quaid and Madeleine Stowe. This is also an album with crossover appeal to a jazz audience--especially fans of legendary trumpeter Chet Baker who sounds superb in three astonishingly restored standards: "Tenderly," "You Go To My Head'" and "These Foolish Things." These were taped in Paris, 1956. As for the orchestral cues, they are quintessentially John Barry at his very best--perfectly arranged, extremely atmospheric in a wistfully romantic mode and, yes, sexy. "John Barry connects with the film in a very personal way," says writer-director Willard Carroll, wisely pressing the point: "You can only talk about the music so far and then your gut has to take over." Mark Knowles

    Director; Luc Besson.

    DVD video, Region 4
    Luc Besson takes us on a roller coaster come magic carpet ride in this snappy, inventive movie.
